## Introduction

The African Charter on Democracy, Elections and Governance (ACDEG) represents the African Union's most comprehensive normative instrument on democratic governance. Adopted in 2007 and entering into force in 2012, ACDEG has now been ratified by 34 of 55 AU member states.

## Implementation Progress

Since the Charter's entry into force, measurable progress has been recorded in several areas, particularly electoral governance reform, civic space protections, and anti-corruption institutional development. However, critical implementation gaps remain across all five thematic areas of the Charter.

## Thematic Analysis

### Democracy and Rule of Law
Progress has been uneven. While constitutional frameworks across the continent broadly reflect democratic governance principles, institutional practice frequently diverges from constitutional text — most critically in relation to judicial independence and separation of powers.

### Institutions and Accountability
Public accountability institutions — including supreme audit institutions, anti-corruption agencies, and ombudspersons — have proliferated across Africa. However, institutional independence, resource adequacy, and follow-up on findings remain serious concerns.

### Democratic Culture
The establishment of a genuine culture of democratic governance — where citizens view democracy as the only legitimate system of government — requires sustained civic education investment. Current efforts fall significantly short of what is required.

## Recommendations

This report makes twelve specific recommendations to the African Union, RECs, and member states to accelerate ACDEG implementation, with particular emphasis on the African Peer Review Mechanism as the primary continental accountability instrument.
