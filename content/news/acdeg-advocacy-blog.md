Twelve years have passed since the African Charter on Democracy, Elections and Governance (ACDEG) entered into force in February 2012. In that time, African governance has been transformed — not always in the direction the Charter's drafters hoped.

Coups have returned. Democratic backsliding has accelerated. Civic space has contracted. The promise of AU Agenda 2063's democratic governance aspirations has been tested like never before.

And yet: ACDEG endures. Its normative framework — grounded in African values, responsive to African governance realities, and aligned with the best of international democratic standards — remains the most sophisticated regional governance instrument anywhere in the world.

## What Makes ACDEG Different

Unlike governance instruments developed in other regional contexts, ACDEG was built from the inside out. It reflects decades of African governance experience, the hard lessons of post-independence democratic failures, and the determination of African citizens and civil society to define governance standards on their own terms.

Its comprehensiveness is striking. ACDEG covers democratic institutions, civil society, elections, the rule of law, political culture, unconstitutional changes of government, sanctions mechanisms, and post-conflict reconstruction — all within a single, coherent normative framework.

## The Implementation Gap

The Charter's greatest weakness is not in its text but in its implementation. Of 55 AU member states, only 34 have ratified the Charter. Of those, implementation has been uneven at best.

This implementation gap is not inevitable. It is a political choice — one that ADGH is committed to challenging through evidence-based advocacy, capacity building, and continental engagement.

## The Path Forward

Closing the ACDEG implementation gap requires three things: political will, institutional capacity, and citizens who know their rights. ADGH is committed to contributing to all three.

The future of African democracy depends not on waiting for governments to act, but on building the civic infrastructure that makes democratic governance the only viable option.
